data-pid="jFgZ_OX5">but：</p><blockquote data-pid="3SsVGPNz">The Great Depression had spurred increased state ownership in most Western capitalist countries. This also took place in Germany during the last years of the Weimar Republic.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-40" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[40]</a> However, after the Nazis took power, industries were privatized <i>en masse</i>. Several banks, shipyards, railway lines, shipping lines, welfare organizations, and more were privatized.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-41" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[41]</a> The Nazi government took the stance that enterprises should be in private hands wherever possible.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A2-42" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[42]</a> State ownership was to be avoided unless it was absolutely necessary for rearmament or the war effort, and even in those cases “the Reich often insisted on the inclusion in the contract of an option clause according to which the private firm operating the plant was entitled to purchase it.”<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A2-42" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[42]</a> However, the privatization was &#34;applied within a framework of increasing control of the state over the whole economy through regulation and political interference,&#34;<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-43" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[43]</a> as laid out in the 1933 Act for the Formation of Compulsory Cartels, which gave the government a role in regulating and controlling the cartels that had been earlier formed in the Weimar Republic under the Cartel Act of 1923.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-44" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[44]</a> These had mostly regulated themselves from 1923 to 1933.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-45" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[45]</a><br/>Companies privatized by the Nazis included the four major commercial banks in Germany, which had all come under public ownership during the prior years: <i>Commerz– und Privatbank</i>, <i>Deutsche Bank und Disconto-Gesellschaft</i>, <i>Golddiskontbank</i> and <i>Dresdner Bank</i>.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-46" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[46]</a><a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A2-42" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[42]</a> Also privatized were the <i>Deutsche Reichsbahn</i> (German Railways), at the time the largest single public enterprise in the world, the <i>Vereinigte Stahlwerke A.G.</i> (United Steelworks), the second largest joint-stock company in Germany (the largest was <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IG_Farben" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">IG Farben</a>) and <i>Vereinigte Oberschlesische Hüttenwerke AG</i>, a company controlling all of the metal production in the Upper Silesian coal and steel industry. The government also sold a number of shipbuilding companies, and enhanced private utilities at the expense of municipally owned utilities companies.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-47" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[47]</a> Additionally, the Nazis privatized some public services which had been previously provided by the government, especially social and labor-related services, and these were mainly taken over by organizations affiliated with the Nazi Party that could be trusted to apply Nazi racial policies.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-48" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[48]</a><br/>One of the reasons for the Nazi privatization policy was to cement the partnership between the government and business interests.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-49" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[49]</a> Hitler believed that the lack of a precise economic programme was one of the Nazi Party&#39;s strengths, saying: &#34;The basic feature of our economic theory is that we have no theory at all&#34;.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-50" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[50]</a> Another reason was financial. As the Nazi government faced budget deficits due to its military spending, privatization was one of the methods it used to raise more funds.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-51" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[51]</a> Between the fiscal years 1934–35 and 1937–38, privatization represented 1.4 percent of the German government&#39;s revenues.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-52" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[52]</a> There was also an ideological motivation. Nazi ideology held <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entrepreneurship" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">entrepreneurship</a> in high regard, and “private property was considered a precondition to developing the creativity of members of the German race in the best interest of the people.&#34;<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A1-53" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[53]</a> The Nazi leadership believed that “private property itself provided important incentives to achieve greater cost consciousness, efficiency gains, and technical progress.”<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A1-53" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[53]</a> Adolf Hitler used <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Darwinism" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">Social Darwinist</a> arguments to support this stance, cautioning against “bureaucratic managing of the economy” that would preserve the weak and “represent a burden to the higher ability, industry and value.”<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-54" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[54]</a><br/>The month after being appointed Chancellor, Hitler made a personal appeal to German business leaders to help fund the Nazi Party for the crucial months that were to follow. He argued that they should support him in establishing a dictatorship because &#34;private enterprise cannot be maintained in the age of democracy&#34; and because democracy would allegedly lead to communism.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-FOOTNOTETooze200699-55" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[55]</a> In the following weeks, the Nazi Party received contributions from seventeen different business groups, with the largest coming from <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IG_Farben" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">IG Farben</a> and <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">Deutsche Bank</a>.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-FOOTNOTETooze2006100-56" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[56]</a> Many of these businesses continued to support Hitler even during the war and even profited from persecution of the Jews. The most infamous being firms like Krupp, IG Farben, and some large automobile manufacturers.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-adl.org-57" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[57]</a> Historian Adam Tooze writes that the leaders of German business were therefore &#34;willing partners in the destruction of political pluralism in Germany.&#34;<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-FOOTNOTETooze2006101-58" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[58]</a> In exchange, owners and managers of German businesses were granted unprecedented powers to control their workforce, <a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_bargaining" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">collective bargaining</a> was abolished and wages were frozen at a relatively low level.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-FOOTNOTETooze2006102-59" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[59]</a> Business profits also rose very rapidly, as did corporate investment.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-FOOTNOTETooze2006114-60" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[60]</a><br/>The Nazis granted millions of marks in credits to private businesses.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-61" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[61]</a> Many businessmen had friendly relations to the Nazis,<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-adl.org-57" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" with a capital less than $200,000,” which swiftly effected the collapse of one fifth of all small corporations.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A0-63" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">[63]</a> On July 15, 1933 a law was enacted that imposed compulsory membership in cartels, while by 1934 the Third Reich had mandated a reorganization of all companies and trade associations and formed an alliance with the Nazi regime.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-%3A0-63" class=" wrap external" target="_blank" rel="nofollow The government usually tried to persuade them to join military projects, but firms were worried about overcapacity in case the armaments boom would end. They did not want to commit themselves too much to war-related production for the future.<a href="https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Nazi_Germany%23cite_note-69"
